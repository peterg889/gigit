# Competitive Landscape: Live Entertainment Booking for Small Venues

**Research date:** June 2026
**Scope:** Competitors and adjacent players for a three-sided marketplace connecting (1) bands/musical acts/comedians, (2) sound techs, and (3) small venues (restaurants, coffee shops, bars, breweries) in the US.

**Methodology:** Five parallel research sweeps (band↔venue marketplaces; comedy booking; sound-tech hiring; venue-side workflows and startup postmortems; market sizing and business mechanics), followed by a 3-voter adversarial verification pass on ~30 load-bearing claims. Claims that were refuted or weakened in verification are corrected or flagged below. Self-reported marketing numbers are labeled as such.

---

## 1. Executive summary

**There is no incumbent that owns this market, but there is one well-funded direct competitor (GigFinesse) and one proven model template overseas (GigPig, UK).** The big profitable players (GigSalad, The Bash) monetize *private-event planners* — weddings, corporate parties — not bars and coffee shops with recurring entertainment programming. The recurring-small-venue niche is served by younger, thinner players, and three findings stand out:

1. **The winning business model is venue-pays, artist-free.** Every platform that charged artists to *apply* for gigs (Sonicbids, ReverbNation Opportunities) destroyed artist trust and declined. GigFinesse (US, ~$15M total funding) and GigPig (UK, 100,000+ gigs booked) both keep artists free and monetize venues.
2. **Nobody has integrated the sound-tech side.** No US-native marketplace lets a venue or band book a live sound engineer (with or without PA rig) as part of a gig booking. This is the most defensible novel piece of the proposed product.
3. **Comedy is underserved by platforms.** Bar/brewery comedy nights are booked almost entirely through human producers (Don't Tell Comedy, Brewery Comedy Tour) and personal networks. GigFinesse's current site does not list comedians as a category. A platform treating comedy as a first-class vertical alongside music has no direct competitor.

The graveyard (Sonicbids, GigTown, Gigmor, Jukely) shows the failure modes clearly: charging the supply side, subsidizing liquidity with venture money that never compounds, and underestimating how relationship-driven and leakage-prone recurring local bookings are.

## 2. Direct competitors (recurring gigs at hospitality venues)

### GigFinesse — gigfinesse.com — ACTIVE, the closest US competitor
- NYC-based, founded 2019 by Mir Hwang. Seed of $3.6M (Dec 2022, Bessemer participating) ([Music Ally](https://musically.com/2022/12/08/live-bookings-startup-gigfinesse-raises-3-6m-of-seed-funding/)). Series A closed May 2026 with Bessemer, NJME, NYU Innovation Venture Fund, Cosmic, and Resy/Zola co-founders; press headlines say "$15M Series A," but the announcement body states the round brought **total funding to over $15M** — the round itself was ~$11–12M ([The SaaS News](https://www.thesaasnews.com/news/gigfinesse-raises-15m-series-a/); verified by all 3 fact-check voters).
- Model: free for artists; venue side pays (sales-led, **no published pricing — opacity is itself a finding**). Curated artist roster; artists receive push offers with pre-set pay; payouts via Stripe in 2–5 business days ([gigfinesse.com/artist](https://www.gigfinesse.com/artist)).
- Claims (self-reported): 10,000+ artist roster, 1,000+ events/month; clients include St. Regis, Ritz-Carlton, restaurants, breweries, apartment complexes. Markets: NYC, LA, Nashville, Chicago.
- **Comedy:** historically marketed "musicians, DJs, and comedians," but as of mid-2026 its public categories list musicians, DJs, trivia, bingo, and magicians — **not stand-up comedians** (verification voter 1, direct site check).
- Weaknesses: curated/slow artist intake (Trustpilot complaint of 30+ days unanswered onboarding — [Trustpilot](https://www.trustpilot.com/review/www.gigfinesse.com)); concierge model is ops-heavy; thin organic community footprint for its funding level; no sound-tech side.

### GigPig — gigpig.uk — ACTIVE (UK), the model template
- Free for artists; venues pay **£15/gig pay-as-you-go or monthly subscriptions (£40/£150/£250 tiers)** ([gigpig.uk/venues/pricing](https://www.gigpig.uk/venues/pricing)).
- Homepage now claims **100,000+ gigs booked** (verified June 2026); 20,000+ verified artists; £9M booking value in 2024, forecast £30M+ for 2025 ([Prolific North](https://www.prolificnorth.co.uk/news/gigpig-predicts-30m-tripling-of-2025-bookings-as-more-venues-turn-to-live-music-to-drive-footfall/)). Raised ~$2.8M including £1.3M from Jägermeister's Best Nights VC ([Hypebot](https://www.hypebot.com/hypebot/2024/05/live-music-marketplace-gigpig-raises-1-6m.html)). Expanding to Ireland in 2026.
- **Strategic read:** GigPig proves the exact thesis — hospitality venues will pay a modest per-gig or subscription fee for self-serve artist booking — but has not entered the US. It is both validation and a fast-follow threat.

### ShowSlinger — app.showslinger.com — ACTIVE (US), thin
- Atlanta-founded marketplace where bars/restaurants/venues post gigs and artists receive offers; free artist accounts with submission limits pushing artists to paid tiers (~2 shows/month free per third-party reviews); next-day PayPal payouts ([Performer Mag](https://performermag.com/band-management/booking-gigs-touring/showslinger-the-next-killer-app-for-booking-touring/), [Omari MC review](https://www.omarimc.com/7-gig-booking-sites-that-actually-help-musicians/)). Now leads with ticketing/event management; marketplace is behind login. Note: it charges the **artist** side — the historically losing model.

### Hedlyner — hedlyner.com — ACTIVE (US), very early
- Launched ~Feb 2025; booking software aimed at breweries, wineries, bars, restaurants; bookers post opportunities, artists submit; handles payments and tax documents ([Urban Influencer launch coverage](https://www.theurbaninfluencer.com/article/new-company-hedlyner-debuts)). Pricing/traction not public.

### Open Comedy — opencomedy.com — ACTIVE, comedy-only, free
- The closest thing to a comedy booking marketplace: bookers (clubs, bars) post gigs with pay; comedians apply one-click; lineup builder; auto press kits. Claims 12,000+ comedian profiles and 9,800+ bookings, free for both sides ("No credit card. No fees. No catch.") — marketing claims, no third-party verification, **no funding rounds found** (verified). Monetization unclear; early-stage.

## 3. Private-event marketplaces (adjacent, not direct)

### GigSalad — gigsalad.com — ACTIVE, profitable, bootstrapped (2007)
- Sides: 110K+ performers/vendors across 500+ categories vs. **private-event planners** (parties, weddings, corporate) — not venue programming.
- Fees (verified on primary sources): free tier 5% booking fee; paid tiers 2.5% — Pro $359/yr, Featured $479/yr ([membership pricing](https://help.gigsalad.com/article/148-membership-pricing), [provider fees](https://help.gigsalad.com/article/97-vendor-service-fees)). Planners also pay a service fee.
- Scale: $113M cumulative paid to performers since 2007; ~$12.8M in the latest reported year across ~110K providers — i.e., **very low per-artist liquidity** ([Hypebot](https://www.hypebot.com/hypebot/2025/04/gigsalad-booked-live-gigs-worth-113-million-for-indie-artists.html)).
- Reputation: "bottom-of-the-barrel leads… pits vendors against each other"; consensus is it serves beginners, not acts charging $500+ ([SideHusl](https://sidehusl.com/gigsalad/)).
- Has a Sound Technician category (avg $300–$900/event, verified) — the only marketplace presence sound techs have today.

### The Bash (ex-GigMasters) — thebash.com — ACTIVE, owned by The Knot Worldwide
- Annual vendor membership (~$99–$219 depending on tier/promo; exact current pricing is behind signup — flagged soft in verification) **plus 5% booking fee, $20 minimum** (verified on [The Bash's own page](https://info.thebash.com/booking-fee-update)).
- EventPay caps in-platform payments at $2,000 deposit / $5,000 balance; booking guarantee covers **only** in-system payments ([guarantee](https://www.thebash.com/guarantee)) — an instructive design choice for fighting leakage.
- Liquidity check (third party): the top acoustic band had only 5 verified bookings in 2024 ([SideHusl](https://sidehusl.com/thebash/)).
- National average comedian booking ~$460 (platform data).

**Takeaway:** both incumbents prove performers will pay subscriptions + 2.5–5% take where leads exist, but neither touches recurring venue programming, and both suffer race-to-the-bottom quote dynamics that performers resent ([Christopher Grace](https://www.christophergracelive.com/post/the-hidden-costs-sites-like-the-bash-and-gigsalad-why-performers-and-clients-both-lose)).

## 5. Directories and DIY tools (the status quo to displace)

- **Indie on the Move** (indieonthemove.com): venue contact database + classifieds for touring DIY bands; free/$6.99/mo tiers, $0.25/venue mass-pitch emails. No payments or booking rails — booking happens over email.
- **ReverbNation Gig Finder** (BandLab-owned): 600K+ venue listing database + pay-to-submit "Opportunities." Long-standing musician resentment of submission tollbooths ([TalkBass](https://www.talkbass.com/threads/the-truth-about-artist-%C2%93gig-finder%C2%94-services.776508/)).
- **Sonicbids** (sonicbids.com): the cautionary tale — see §7. Still alive but diminished; $19.99/mo Gig Seeker Pro + $5–$25 per submission.
- **Bandsintown**: 100M registered fans, ~700K artists; advertising/promo layer, *no* booking function — but a natural syndication target for event promotion.
- **Comedy mic-finders:** Badslava ("Call Before You Haul!" — chronically stale), Open Mic Finder (App Store reviewers: data "old," app "comatose"), FireMics, city spreadsheets. Comedy discovery infrastructure is decayed — corroborates the opportunity.

## 6. Managed promoters (different business, instructive economics)

- **Sofar Sounds**: NOT a venue-booking marketplace — it is a promoter that owns the ticket revenue and uses spaces as free hosts. Raised ~$25M (2019, Battery + USV). In 2019 it paid bands ~$100 flat for 25-minute sets while retaining $1,100–$1,600+/show ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2019/05/21/how-sofar-sounds-works/)); a NY State DOL investigation ended in a **$460,357.50 settlement to 654 unpaid workers** ([NYS DOL](https://dol.ny.gov/news/new-york-state-department-labor-investigation-leads-654-concert-workers-recovering-460000)). Current pay: $100/$125/$150 attendance-tiered minimums, claimed ~70% of net profits, $30M+ paid to artists since 2020 (company-reported). Lesson: **opaque spreads between what the room generates and what artists get is a durable PR and legal liability; pay transparency is a wedge.**
- **Don't Tell Comedy** (founded 2017): hires local comics to scout venues and produce pop-up shows; ~5M YouTube subscribers now ([Axios](https://www.axios.com/2024/08/26/dont-tell-comedy-viral-stars), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2024/09/08/entertainment/dont-tell-comedy-stand-up-cec/index.html)). Proof that non-comedy-club spaces + local comic-producers is a scalable formula — executed with humans, not software.
- **The Brewery Comedy Tour** (Herron Entertainment): claims 1,500+ brewery stops (marketing, corroborated by many independent brewery event pages). Turnkey packaged shows: the venue never curates talent.

## 7. The graveyard — verified failure modes

| Company | What happened | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| **Sonicbids** | Artist-pays model ($119/yr + $5–$10 per application); Vice investigation found low-value gigs and auto-billing complaints; Sled Island left in 2013 (NXNE threatened to but walked it back); sold to Backstage for ~$15M (2013), divested to Advance Music Technologies (2024) ([Vice](https://www.vice.com/en/article/is-sonicbids-problematic/), [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2013/01/30/backstage-acquires-music-promotion-startup-sonicbids-for-15m-to-build-a-linkedin-for-creatives/), [CelebrityAccess](https://celebrityaccess.com/2024/07/09/musician-epk-platform-sonicbids-sold-to-advance-music-technologies/)) | **Never charge artists to apply.** It generates revenue while corroding the trust that gives the platform value. |
| **GigTown** | "Uber for local music," San Diego 2015; ex-Qualcomm president Steve Altman put in $2M; 13% commission while *subsidizing* artist payments ($5–$10 per fan check-in); never expanded beyond region; sold to two local musicians April 2020 ([Entrepreneur](https://www.entrepreneur.com/science-technology/this-app-brings-musicians-party-planners-and-venues/248011), [SDBJ](https://www.sdbj.com/featured/gigtown-books-a-post-pandemic-reboot/)) | **Subsidized liquidity doesn't compound.** Local gig marketplaces must reach organic liquidity city-by-city. |
| **Gigmor** | LA, 2014; claimed 80K artists/5K venues; ran a Wefunder community round, then an April 2025 GoFundMe seeking $14K that raised **$465** ([GoFundMe](https://www.gofundme.com/f/help-gigmor-build-a-recommendation-engine-with-AI)) | Registration counts ≠ liquidity. Zombie status despite a decade of operation. |
| **Jukely** | "Netflix of concerts" subscription; capped "unlimited" plans in 2017; site now dead (quiet death, likely COVID-era; end date unverified) | Demand-side subscription for live events didn't survive. |
| **SoundBetter** | Audio-pro marketplace; acquired by Spotify (Sept 2019), **sold back to its founders (Oct 2021)** ([Spotify Newsroom](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2019-09-12/spotify-announces-acquisition-of-global-audio-services-marketplace-soundbetter/), [SoundBetter](https://soundbetter.com/site/soundbetter-founders-buy-company-back-from-spotify)) | Even Spotify couldn't scale an audio-services marketplace; niche services marketplaces are hard. |
| Sector-wide | 18 of 30 startups on a 2013 MIDEM "hot list" were dead within ~3 years ([Cuepoint](https://medium.com/cuepoint/the-music-startup-meltdown-cd2f7a38e939)) | Causes: founders without industry knowledge, overestimating demand, "limited monetization potential among hobbyist musicians." |

## 8. The sound-tech side: a verified gap

- **No US-native marketplace bundles live sound techs + PA rigs for small venues/bands.** An adversarial counterexample search (voter 3) found only GigHeaven — UK-based, general-events, serving the US with ~209 PA providers and "fully operated packages with a sound technician" — plus GigSalad's generic category and Thumbtack/Bark lead-gen. Nothing US-native and bar/band-dedicated exists.
- **LASSO** (lasso.io) is the serious player in event crew, but it's B2B staffing for production companies, and its crew are **W-2 employees of LASSO** (verified on LASSO's own FAQ) — a completely different shape.
- Practitioner economics (anecdotal, consistent): labor-only $100–$250 for a 3–4hr bar gig; tech + own PA $150–$400+; full production $300–$900 (GigSalad platform data: $300–$900 avg). SoundGirls' floor guidance: $100/gig minimum.
- Pain points: discovery is network-gated ("the hardest part about being a sound engineer is finding work" — [Sound Design Live](https://www.sounddesignlive.com/opening-marketplace-sound-engineers/)); slow payment (LASSO and AVLancer both market fast pay as a differentiator); many small venues have **no house PA**, so the bring-your-own-PA burden routinely falls on bands (consistent forum consensus, no quantitative survey exists).

**Gig pay planning anchors** (practitioner-reported, no official source exists — verified as consistent across forums):
- Solo acoustic at coffee shop/restaurant: $50–$150/set (+tips)
- Bar band: $100–$350/musician; 4-piece commonly **$400–$800/night**
- Comedy: hosts $25–$100, features $50–$300, headliners $100–$200/45-min set; venue guide norm: $5–$10 cover, "$300–$750 for a one-hour performer"
- Sound tech: $150–$400 with PA for a bar gig

## 10. Operational & regulatory facts a platform must handle (all verified)

- **1099-K:** post-OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025), the federal TPSO threshold reverted to **>$20,000 AND >200 transactions**, retroactive to 2025 ([IRS](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-issues-faqs-on-form-1099-k-threshold-under-the-one-big-beautiful-bill-dollar-limit-reverts-to-20000)). Several states still require $600–$1,000 (MA, MD, NJ, etc.) — collect W-9s early.
- **Payments:** Stripe explicitly does not offer escrow; the marketplace pattern is **Stripe Connect with manual payouts** — charge the venue at booking, hold in balance (up to ~90 days), release to performer after the gig ([Stripe docs](https://docs.stripe.com/connect/manual-payouts)).
- **Contracts:** industry norm is ~50% deposit at signing (artist keeps it on client cancellation), balance due day-of; full fee inside ~48hrs; tech/hospitality riders standard ([Dave Ruch](https://daveruch.com/advice/live-performance-contracts/)).
- **Insurance:** venues commonly require performer COIs naming them additional insured; musician GL policies start ~$199/yr ([Insurance Canopy](https://www.insurancecanopy.com/entertainment-insurance/performers/musicians)).
- **PRO licensing (venue's obligation):** a small venue with live music typically pays on the order of $1,500/yr combined ASCAP+BMI+SESAC (ranges $300–$500/PRO; up to ~$10K for larger rooms; GMR is a fourth bill); statutory damages up to $150,000/work; ASCAP actively sues small venues. Exact per-PRO minimums fluctuate yearly (BMI ~$294–$415 depending on size/year) — treat specific figures as approximate ([RestaurantOwner.com](https://www.restaurantowner.com/public/Paying-the-Piper-Restaurant-Music-Licensing-Fees.cfm), [BMI](https://www.bmi.com/licensing/entry/bars_and_restaurants)). **An original-music-only or licensing-guidance feature is a real venue-side value-add.**

## 11. Marketplace design lessons (from the literature + this category's history)

1. **Take-rate benchmarks:** modest rake + high volume beats extraction (Gurley, [A Rake Too Far](https://abovethecrowd.com/2013/04/18/a-rake-too-far-optimal-platformpricing-strategy/)); comps: Airbnb ~15% blended, Upwork ~17.7%, Etsy 6.5%, GigSalad 2.5–5%+subs, GigPig £15/gig flat.
2. **Disintermediation is the central structural risk.** Recurring local services (a band landing a monthly residency) are the classic leakage case ([a16z Marketplace Glossary](https://a16z.com/the-marketplace-glossary/), [Hagiu & Wright](https://platformchronicles.substack.com/p/platform-leakage)). Counters: make staying on-platform worth it (payments, contracts, dispute protection, insurance verification, scheduling, promotion), keep the rake low or flat-fee, and consider SaaS monetization of the venue relationship instead of taxing every transaction. The Bash's "guarantee only covers in-system payments" is the standard carrot-shaped stick.
3. **Optimize "Happy GMV"** — retention/rebooking, not raw GMV ([Sarah Tavel](https://sarahtavel.medium.com/the-hierarchy-of-marketplaces-introduction-and-level-1-983995aa218e)). For this category: venue slot fill-rate and artist rebooking rate are the metrics that matter.
4. **City-by-city density, not breadth.** Every failure (GigTown, Gigmor) spread thin; GigPig won Manchester first. Liquidity is hyper-local.
5. **Artist-pay transparency is a wedge** (Sofar's lasting PR damage proves the inverse).

## 12. Strategic implications for Gigit

1. **Differentiate on the two genuinely open flanks:** (a) sound techs as a first-class booking object — nobody does this; (b) comedy as a first-class vertical — only human producers and one unfunded free site do this.
2. **Business model:** free for artists and sound techs, venue-pays (GigPig-style flat per-gig fee or subscription). Never charge to apply. Payment processing margin + venue SaaS as secondary revenue.
3. **Go-to-market:** single metro; seed supply via the existing scene (the Don't Tell model — recruit the local comics/bookers who already produce nights); target venue types where the status quo is weakest (breweries, coffee shops without booking relationships).
4. **The moat is workflow, not discovery:** contracts, deposits/escrow-like payment release, COI collection, replacement-on-cancellation, recurring residency scheduling, promotion syndication. Discovery alone is a feature; the workflow is the business.
5. **Fast-follow threats:** GigFinesse expanding self-serve down-market, GigPig entering the US.
